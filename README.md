# Global Cyber Security Threat & Fraud Analysis
### This Repository has Global Cyber Security Threat & Fraud Analysis project


_"Know Your Enemy Before It Knows Your System."_


## Project Overview
This project provides a comprehensive analysis of global cyber security threats, fraud incidents, and their financial and operational impacts. The dataset spans multiple years (2015–2024), industries, and attack types, offering deep insights into how cyber risks evolve and affect organizations worldwide.

This project aims to help organizations, security teams, and decision-makers understand:
    - The scale and impact of cyber attacks
    - Key attack sources and vulnerabilities
    - Financial and operational risks
    - Trends in cybersecurity incidents over time
    - Effectiveness of defense mechanisms
The dashboard transforms complex cybersecurity datasets into meaningful visual insights that support strategic security planning and risk mitigation.


## Objectives of the Project
  - Analyze global cyber attack trends from 2015–2024
  - Identify major attack sources and vulnerability types
  - Measure financial losses caused by cyber incidents
  - Track affected users and attack frequency
  - Evaluate defense mechanisms adopted by organizations
  - Generate actionable insights for management and security teams

  - Total Financial Loss: $151.48K Million

  - Industries Impacted: 7
  - Time Period: 2015 – 2024

# Key Insights
1) Financial Loss by Country — UK Leads, But No One Is Safe
    - The UK recorded the highest financial losses at ~$17K Million, followed closely by Germany and Brazil at ~$16K Million each.
    - Australia, Japan, France, USA, Russia, and India all cluster tightly around $15K Million — indicating a global, borderless threat environment.
    - China records the lowest at ~$14K Million, potentially reflecting stronger state-level cyber controls or underreporting.
    - Actionable: No country is significantly safer than another. A global cybersecurity posture — not a country-by-country patchwork — is the only effective defence.  

2) Security Vulnerability Types — Zero-Day Is the Biggest Door
    - Zero-day vulnerabilities account for the highest attack count (785), followed by Social Engineering (747), Unpatched Software (738), and Weak Passwords (730).
    - The tight range (730–785) across all four vulnerability types tells a sobering story: attackers exploit everything equally well, meaning all four gaps must be closed simultaneously.
    - Actionable: A layered security approach that addresses patching cycles, password policy, employee training, and threat intelligence simultaneously is non-negotiable.

3) Attack Source — Nation-State Actors Are the Costliest Threat
    - Nation-state actors caused the most financial damage at $40K Million, followed by Unknown Sources ($39K Million), Insider Threats ($37K Million), and Hacker Groups ($35K Million).
    - The $5K Million gap between nation-state and hacker group losses underscores the sophisticated, well-funded nature of state-sponsored attacks.
    - Unknown sources at $39K Million represent a critical blind spot — nearly as damaging as nation-states but with no attribution for targeted response.
    - Actionable: Intelligence sharing between governments and the private sector is essential to attribute and deter nation-state and unknown source attacks.

4) 10-Year Trend (2015–2024) — Steady Escalation with Alarming Spikes
    - Both financial losses and total affected users show a consistent upward trajectory from 2015 to 2024.
    - Visible spikes in the trend line align with well-documented global ransomware waves (WannaCry, NotPetya, REvil) and large-scale phishing campaigns.
    - The volume of affected users has grown to near-plateau levels (~150M per year), suggesting attackers have maximized reach and are now focusing on increasing the financial yield per attack.
    - Actionable: Cybersecurity budgets must grow proportionally with the threat curve — flat or declining security investments in a rising threat environment is an organizational liability.

5) Industry Focus — Government Is the Primary Target
    - With Government as a filterable target industry and 7 industries covered, the dashboard makes clear that public sector organizations face disproportionate threat exposure.
    - Industries like Healthcare, Finance, and Energy (traditionally high-value targets) require tailored cyber resilience programs due to the life-critical or economically systemic nature of their operations.



# Business Recommendations (Recommendations for Management)
  - Strengthen Vulnerability Management: Enforce patching cycles, password policies, and regular audits to reduce exposure.
  - Invest in AI Security: Expand AI-based detection and automated response systems to counter evolving threats.
  - Sector-Specific Protection: Prioritize resilience programs for Government and Healthcare, the most targeted industries.
  - Global Collaboration: Share intelligence across borders to mitigate nation-state and unknown source attacks.
  - User Awareness Programs: Scale training initiatives to reduce risks from phishing and social engineering.
  - Balanced Defense Portfolio: Combine traditional tools (antivirus, VPN, encryption) with modern AI-driven solutions for layered protection.
